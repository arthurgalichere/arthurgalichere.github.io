Arthur Galichère

Assistant Professor (Teaching Focussed)

In a few words

I am an Assistant Professor at the University of Warwick, where I lecture on the core 2nd year undergraduate Macroeconomics module and the core MSc module Advanced Macroeconomics.

I joined the University of Warwick as a Teaching Fellow in Macroeconomics in 2021 and received my PhD in Economics from the University of Glasgow in 2022.

My research lies at the intersection of macroeconomics and finance, with a particular focus on:

  • Business cycles
  • Asset price bubbles
  • Macroeconomic policies
  • Financial stability

About me

I am an Assistant Professor at the University of Warwick studying macroeconomics. My main research interests in this field focus on themes related to financial crises and macroeconomic policies, such as asset pricing, monetary policy, heterogeneous investments, financial intermediation and stability. I design theoretical models and apply empirical methods to evaluate the effects of asset price bubbles on the economy to provide policy recommendations. My job market paper studies the dynamic ownership of bubbles and its effects on the real economy and financial stability. In contrast, my current projects focus on the Bayesian estimation of DSGE models with stock market bubbles and nominal rigidities.

My profound interest in economics was awakened by a sequence of economic events that changed the lives of many. The first main event that sparked my curiosity was the burst of the subprime mortgage bubble. I was young, but still, I remember the news being incessantly focused on the financial crisis and the great uncertainty it brought to people. At that time, I was wondering what caused such a situation and how to remedy it.

This curiosity led me to study economics after my Baccalaureate. I started my undergraduate studies at the University of Caen in France and met many great people there. At the same moment, the European debt crisis was in full swing and raised many interrogations in the minds of Europeans. I had the chance to have a very eloquent macroeconomic professor who was narrating his understanding of the crisis, and made me think about what would be the best macroeconomic policy.

After my undergraduate studies, I went abroad for one year in an exchange programme with Texas A&M University in the US. I discovered a very different culture and educational system, where I learnt many new things. During this year, my interest was focused on unconventional monetary policies, which were the themes of my dissertation on the evolution of the monetary policies of the ECB and the Fed since the beginning of the financial crisis. These cultural and academic experiences were very beneficial to me, and this was the moment I decided I wanted to do research in economics.

Before starting my PhD, I wanted to acquire a better understanding of the research tools and techniques required for economic analyses. Therefore, I went to Glasgow and started my MRes in economics. Since then, I have learnt a lot about modern macroeconomic theory and economics. I also met very interesting and supportive people through my MRes and PhD.

I joined the University of Warwick in September 2021 as a Teaching Fellow in Macroeconomics, near the end of my PhD. During my first year at Warwick, I taught seminars and supervised dissertations. This was a great experience, but it was very intense as I was still finishing my PhD, which I completed in June 2022. After this first hectic academic year, I was thrilled to be offered the role of lecturer for the second-year core Macroeconomics module. I was particularly excited about this opportunity as the module focuses on the foundations of advanced macroeconomic research - a subject that I am extremely passionate about, and that aligns with my own research interests.

After three years of dedicated work, I was promoted to Assistant Professor (Teaching Focussed) in 2024. This marked an exciting new chapter for me, as I have also been entrusted with leading and teaching the Advanced Macroeconomics optional core module for the MSc in Economics, a truly exceptional module. The module covers everything from data analysis and economic theory to the development and simulation of new macroeconomic models. My goal in designing this course was to ensure that, by the end, students are fully equipped to become independent macroeconomic analysts or to pursue further studies, such as an MRes or PhD.

In 2025, I won the Warwick Awards for Teaching Excellence (WATE) for the Faculty of Social Sciences. The jury recognised my “innovation, impact, and dedication to enhancing the student experience”. I feel truly honoured by this recognition and very grateful to my students for their nomination and kind feedback. It is deeply rewarding to know that my teaching has made a meaningful difference to their learning. I would also like to thank the WATE jury for this honour, and my colleagues at Warwick for their continued support and inspiration. I do not believe this achievement would have been possible without them.

Nowadays, I am enjoying teaching macroeconomics and doing my research.
