Research

At the intersection of macroeconomics and finance, my research develops and estimates theoretical models to explain business cycle fluctuations and financial market phenomena. I study how financing activities, shaped by market structure, financial frictions, and investor behaviour, affect credit, aggregate investment, and economic activity within general equilibrium frameworks. My work seeks to improve our understanding of financial crises and risk-premium-driven recessions, while informing the design of monetary and macroprudential policies.

Working Papers

When Banks Ride the Bubble: Financial Stability and Real Activity
My paper studies the implications of the dynamic ownership of risky asset price bubbles between banks and retail investors for financial stability and real activity. The analysis shows that the severity of banking crises and recessions depends on both the exposure of individual banks to the bubble and the overall contamination of the banking sector. The paper also highlights that the early stages of bubble growth increase the financial vulnerability of the economy, as banks increasingly invest in the bubble during this period. While stricter banking supervision can reduce the effects of a bursting bubble, it may also weaken banks’ resilience to economic shocks.
Stock Market Bubbles and Monetary policy: a Bayesian Analysis
This paper develops and estimates a DSGE model with stock market bubbles and nominal rigidities using Bayesian methods. Bubbles emerge through a positive feedback loop mechanism supported by self-fulfilling beliefs. Their movements, driven by a sentiment shock, significantly contribute to fluctuations in investment, output, and inflation.

Work in Progress

Stock Market Dynamics in the RBC Framework
This paper develops a DSGE model to study stock market dynamics. A key novelty of the framework is the introduction of firm exit and equity issuance by new entrants.
Asset Price Bubbles and Monetary Policy: Deflate the Bubble?
This paper studies monetary policy in a New Keynesian model with asset price bubbles. It shows that monetary policy targeting asset prices can partially deflate an asset price bubble and affects the way the bubble is financed.
